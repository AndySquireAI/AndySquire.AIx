Physician-as-Pilot: A Regulatory Position Paper

Intended Audience

This document is written for:
- U.S. FDA reviewers and Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) experts
- European Union MDR and UK MHRA regulatory authorities
- Clinical AI governance scholars and academic evaluators, including Harvard Medical School capstone tutors and faculty engaged in clinical AI oversight and safety

Purpose

This paper proposes a clear regulatory boundary model for non-clinical AI systems in healthcare. The Physician-as-Pilot model is designed to preserve physician decisional authority, avoid unintended SaMD classification, and enable safe, auditable AI assistance without autonomous clinical action.

Regulatory Context and Problem Statement

The proliferation of AI in healthcare creates regulatory ambiguity between cognitive assistance and clinical decision-making. This ambiguity can lead to human authority erosion and boundary creep into SaMD without appropriate regulatory oversight. This paper proposes a model to address these challenges by establishing clear, enforceable boundaries for non-clinical AI systems.

Explicit Non-SaMD Boundary Conditions

The Physician-as-Pilot model for non-clinical systems (Phase I) is explicitly constrained to preclude medical device functionality. The system is architecturally prohibited from performing the following functions:

  • Diagnosis, prognosis, or treatment recommendation.
  • Clinical assessment, triage, or risk scoring.
  • Generation of clinical alerts or time-critical alarms.

All AI outputs are non-directive, and all clinical interpretation and action remain exclusively human-executed.

Escalation & Consent Model

Allowed (Phase I)

  • The system may detect “authority ambiguity” or “user distress language” solely to trigger an internal halt + suggestion workflow (e.g., “Would you like to contact a caregiver?”), but it cannot initiate any external outreach without explicit user authorization.

Prohibited (Phase I)

  • No automatic alerting, triage, prioritization, routing, or contacting clinicians/caregivers.

Consent is Explicit and Transactional

  • Each escalation requires affirmative user confirmation
  • Consent is captured per action, not assumed
  • Consent state is logged immutably

Escalation Pathways

  • User → AI → Human caregiver / doctor's office
  • AI does not interpret, filter, or prioritize escalation content
Auditability & Evidence

Immutable Audit Logging

  • All interactions logged
  • All consent events recorded
  • All escalations timestamped
  • Logs are tamper-resistant

Audit Readiness

  • Logs available for regulatory, sponsor, or compliance review
  • Human actions traceable to individuals
  • System behavior reproducible and inspectable
Safety Architecture (Safety OS)
Safety OS Control Surfaces (Phase I)
Consent state versioned; scope/duration/revocation
Authority holder user/caregiver only
Permitted actions conversation, reminders, user-requested contact drafting
Hard refusals diagnosis/triage/treatment/medication guidance
Escalation mode suggestion only; external action requires explicit “yes”
Audit artifacts event log + audit summary + redacted export

Deterministic System Behavior

  • Rule-defined routing only
  • No probabilistic clinical output
  • No hidden decision layers

Hard Constraints Enforced

  • Clinical intent requests are refused, not redirected
  • Boundary violations are blocked at system level
  • No model override of governance rules

Why Governance Must Precede Model Performance

  • Transparency: what data the system uses and what it never uses in Phase I
  • Reproducibility: consistent behavior through fixed rules + exportable audit logs
  • Generalizability: governance controls are hardware/model-agnostic; they don’t “break” under dataset shift
  • Calibration/thresholds: Phase II introduces physician-defined thresholds (care envelope) rather than arbitrary defaults

Phase I → Phase II Boundary Rule (Canonical)

Phase I ends and Phase II governance is required the moment the system is expected to initiate or prioritize care actions (alerts, structured safety questioning, clinical routing, or protocol-driven escalation) without case-by-case user instruction—because that requires a clinician-signed care envelope.
