Physician-as-Pilot™

A governance framework for AI-mediated care where clinicians retain ultimate authority.

Unclear clinical authority in AI-mediated care creates safety and liability gaps. Physician-as-Pilot closes those gaps by formalizing explicit human authority, auditable constraints, and enforced escalation pathways—so AI can support care without silently taking control.

Definition

Physician-as-Pilot is a governance framework for AI-mediated care in which clinicians retain ultimate authority, situational awareness, and override control—while AI systems function as copilots, supporting but not replacing clinical judgment.

It is a "control doctrine" for AI in care settings: define who holds authority, what the system is allowed to do, what it must never do, and how it escalates to a human when uncertainty or risk increases.

Illustrative authority flow: Physician intent → Safety OS enforcement → AI execution → Patient context.

Why This Framework Was Developed

As AI systems move into unsupervised and semi-supervised care environments, existing “human-in-the-loop” models fail to specify who is accountable when automation acts. Physician-as-Pilot was developed to close that governance gap by formalizing clinical authority, override control, and escalation responsibility across the AI lifecycle. This anchors the framework to a problem, not an ideology.

Regulators and standard-setting bodies increasingly emphasize explicit human accountability, not implied oversight.

What Physician-as-Pilot Is Not

Physician-as-Pilot is not:

  • Clinical guidance, a protocol, or a care pathway
  • A diagnostic system, triage tool, or autonomous clinician substitute
  • A claim that any specific implementation is regulated, approved, or clinically validated

Physician-as-Pilot is governance: it defines authority, boundaries, escalation, and accountability.

Core Governance Commitments

  • Clinicians retain final authority over AI-mediated actions and decisions.
  • AI operates only within explicit, predefined boundaries set by clinicians and policy.
  • Overrides and disengagement mechanisms are always available and usable.
  • Escalation triggers are explicit and enforced (uncertainty, risk, abnormal context, user request, system constraint).
  • Accountability is attributable and reviewable, supported by logging and auditability.
  • Consent and permissions are explicit, scoped, and revocable.

Core Principles

The following principles ensure that the commitments above are operationally visible and enforceable:

Human authority is explicit, not implied

The clinician's role as final decision-maker is architecturally enforced, not assumed.

AI recommendations must be inspectable

Every AI output includes reasoning that can be reviewed, questioned, and overridden.

Override is a feature, not a failure

The system is designed expecting clinician intervention, not treating it as an exception.

Responsibility cannot be delegated to software

Accountability remains with humans. AI is a tool, never a decision-maker.

Relationship to Safety OS

Physician-as-Pilot defines the governance commitments. Safety OS is the systems architecture that implements those commitments through deterministic control, state management, consent tracking, constraints enforcement, and auditability.

Reference Architecture (Illustrative, Not Prescriptive)

Below is a representative pattern showing how governance layers can be instantiated—illustrated using the Home Companion model.

Important: This is one possible architecture. Physician-as-Pilot does not depend on this specific stack, and no clinical functions are implied absent appropriate regulatory approvals.

Architecture Legend

  • User Interaction: voice, listening, conversation
  • Agent Orchestrator: consent, state, escalation, logging, constraints
  • Intent Router: predefined intents only (routine, social, support, help request)
  • Reasoning Engine: LLM is replaceable and non-authoritative (language generation only)
  • Human Handoff: trusted contact → caregiver → clinician (as appropriate)

What It Will NOT Do

  • Diagnose
  • Decide clinically
  • Act without explicit consent

Illustrative Governance Sequencing (Example Only)

  • Phase I: Consent-based, non-medical support under Safety OS constraints (Home Companion).
  • Phase II: Clinician-supervised escalation with explicit accountability.
  • Phase III: Regulatory-aligned clinical AI with enforced authority boundaries.

This roadmap is illustrative and not an endorsement of any specific product timeline, clinical claim, or regulatory status.
