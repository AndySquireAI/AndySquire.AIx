Clinical AI Governance

Authority, Boundaries, Enforcement, Auditability

Clinical AI governance is not about model intelligence.

It is about who holds authority, how limits are enforced, and whether accountability is provable.

This page describes the governance architecture that enables safe progression from non-medical AI to regulated clinical systems — without premature delegation of authority.

Governance Framework Context

PatientCentricCare structures AI deployment through three governance phases:

Phase I — Non-Medical AI

No clinical framing or inference.

Phase II — SaMD-Ready, Physician-Supervised AI

Escalation-only execution under defined clinical authority.

Phase III — Regulated Clinical AI (SaMD)

Licensed inference under regulatory approval.

Invariant: Clinical authority is never delegated to AI — at any phase.

Governance Is Infrastructure, Not Policy

Most AI governance today relies on:

  • Guidelines
  • Training
  • Monitoring after deployment

These approaches fail in regulated contexts.

PatientCentricCare treats governance as enforced infrastructure, not documentation.

Controls are applied before execution, not audited after harm.

Core Pillars of Clinical AI Governance

1. Authority Inventory

Every decision an AI system could influence is:

  • Explicitly defined
  • Assigned to a human authority holder
  • Approved before execution is permitted

No undefined decisions. No implied authority.

2. Boundaries & Permissioning

AI execution is constrained to explicit authority envelopes.

  • Permitted actions are defined in advance
  • Anything outside scope is refused by default
  • Expansion requires human re-authorization

This prevents silent scope creep and autonomy leakage.

3. Enforcement at Runtime

Governance is enforced through a control layer (Safety OS):

  • Policy-constrained actions
  • Mandatory refusal when boundaries are exceeded
  • Structured escalation to humans

Controls are enforced at runtime, not by post-hoc review.

4. Auditability & Accountability

All system behavior is continuously logged:

  • Actions
  • Refusals
  • Escalations
  • Authority assignments

Logs are immutable, time-stamped, and attributable — supporting regulatory traceability, incident review, and post-market surveillance.

Regulatory Alignment (Without Premature Claims)

The governance architecture supports expectations found in:

  • FDA SaMD guidance
  • EU MDR (clinical evaluation, PMS, traceability)
  • Quality management and audit requirements

This alignment does not imply regulatory approval.
It ensures systems are structurally prepared for regulated pathways.

What This Governance Model Is Not

  • Not autonomous clinical decision-making
  • Not a claim of FDA / MDR approval
  • Not a consumer AI product
  • Not a replacement for licensed clinicians
  • It is a control architecture for safe, accountable AI execution.

Governance in Practice

In operational terms, this governance model ensures:

  • Named human authority for every clinical action
  • Enforced refusal outside approved scope
  • Continuous audit trails
  • Structured escalation pathways
  • Proven safety behavior before expanded autonomy

Capability must follow governance — not the other way around.

Clinical AI can only scale responsibly when authority is explicit, boundaries are enforced, and accountability is provable.
