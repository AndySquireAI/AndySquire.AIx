Clinical AI Governance: The Physician-as-Pilot Model

Key Definitions

Non-Clinical System / Consumer Health Companion: A voice interface that does not perform clinical decisions or care recommendations and is limited to education, companionship, and caregiver communication.

Clinical Authority System: A governance architecture where a licensed physician holds final clinical decision authority, and AI executes only within boundaries defined by that authority.

Governance Maturity: The intentional progression of risk controls from non-clinical to clinical authority governance systems.

Regulatory Context (Non-Normative)

  • This framework does not by itself constitute a regulated medical device.
  • System maturity progression is designed to enable SaMD-ready governance infrastructure.
  • All clinical authority resides with licensed clinicians under this model; the AI itself does not make autonomous clinical decisions.

Executive Summary

This page presents a Clinical AI Governance Ecosystem for patient-facing AI, illustrating a structured progression from non-clinical consumer support tools to a physician-governed clinical authority model. Phase I describes a non-clinical home companion designed for education, companionship, and caregiver communication, explicitly excluding diagnosis, triage, or treatment decisions. Phase II introduces a Physician-as-Pilot governance architecture in which clinical authority, escalation criteria, and safety boundaries are defined and held by licensed clinicians. The ecosystem emphasizes human-centric design, deterministic escalation, and automated, tamper-evident audit trails to support traceability, accountability, and SaMD-ready governance without implying autonomous clinical decision-making.

Why Most Clinical AI Governance Fails Regulators

Current approaches break down because they:

  • Treat "human-in-the-loop" as a UI feature, not a legal authority model
  • Allow AI systems to influence care without defining escalation thresholds
  • Conflate usability, intelligence, and clinical responsibility
  • Rely on post-hoc monitoring instead of pre-emptive constraints

As a result, many systems unintentionally drift into de facto SaMD behavior without the governance structures required to support it.

Regulatory risk emerges not from intent, but from ambiguity.

What "Physician-as-Pilot" Means in Practice

The Physician-as-Pilot model assigns continuous clinical authority to a licensed clinician across all deployment phases.

AI systems are constrained to:

  • Protocol-bound roles
  • Non-autonomous execution
  • Deterministic escalation
  • Human override at all times

The AI may assist, observe, summarize, or coordinate — but it never holds clinical authority.

Authority is explicit, inspectable, and auditable.

Explicit Non-SaMD Boundaries (Phase I)

In Phase I deployments, AI systems are explicitly prohibited from:

  • Diagnosis
  • Prognosis
  • Treatment recommendation
  • Autonomous clinical decision-making

Permitted behaviors are:

  • Conversational support
  • Contextual observation
  • Non-medical reminders
  • Structured check-ins

All interpretation and action remain human-executed. This preserves regulatory clarity while enabling safe early deployment.

Deterministic Escalation and Halt Conditions

Escalation is not discretionary or probabilistic.

The model defines pre-registered escalation triggers, including:

  • Consent state changes
  • Boundary ambiguity
  • Context uncertainty
  • Protocol deviation
  • System fault

When thresholds are crossed:

  • The system escalates
  • Or halts
  • Or disengages

These actions are automatic, logged, and non-negotiable.

Escalation follows predefined and clinically validated thresholds that trigger human review or clinical action; no escalation happens without predefined criteria and physician oversight.

Safety OS: Governance as Infrastructure

Governance is not policy. It is runtime infrastructure.

The Safety OS enforces:

  • Consent state management
  • Authority attribution
  • Escalation logic
  • Immutable audit logs

This enables:

  • Regulatory review
  • Post-market surveillance
  • Incident reconstruction
  • Responsibility attribution

Safety is designed into the system - not layered on afterward.

Audit trails include automatically generated logs for system interactions, threshold outcomes, and physician actions to support traceability and safety monitoring.

Phased Regulatory Pathway

The model supports a staged pathway:

  • Phase I: Non-medical, non-SaMD AI with explicit governance constraints
  • Phase II: Clinician-supervised, care-adjacent AI with SaMD-ready controls
  • Phase III: Regulated clinical AI subject to jurisdictional approval

Progression depends on governance maturity, not technical capability alone.
