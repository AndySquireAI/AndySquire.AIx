Hybrid Human–Agent Operating Standard

Version 1.0

A Governance Specification for Authority, Boundaries, Enforcement, and Auditability

This standard is a reference model for how governance constructs apply to hybrid human–AI systems. It is not a product feature list or clinical claim.

Purpose and Scope

This Hybrid Human–Agent Operating Standard defines the governance constructs required to safely deploy AI systems in healthcare and care-adjacent contexts without delegating clinical authority to machines.

It specifies how human authority, system boundaries, deterministic enforcement, and auditability must be structured across deployment contexts ranging from non-medical support to regulated clinical AI, including Software as a Medical Device (SaMD).

This document is a reference standard, not a product description, clinical claim, or commercial roadmap.

Design Principle

Clinical authority must never be delegated to AI systems.

AI systems may execute tasks, surface information, or support coordination, but authority to define clinical intent, approve clinical action, and assume accountability must always reside with a licensed human professional or designated human authority.

Failures of healthcare AI typically arise not from insufficient model capability, but from unclear authority boundaries and premature delegation of decision rights. This standard addresses that root cause.

What This Standard Defines

This operating standard specifies:

  • How authority is assigned, scoped, and revoked
  • How boundaries are enforced deterministically (not probabilistically)
  • How escalation occurs without recommendation or inference
  • How audit artifacts are generated and preserved
  • How governance remains invariant across deployment phases

It does not define:

  • Product features
  • Commercial packaging
  • Clinical performance claims
  • Regulatory approval or clearance

Core Governance Constructs

Authority Construct

Defines who is authorized to act, under what scope, and for what duration.

Authority is:

  • Explicitly assigned
  • Versioned
  • Revocable
  • Logged

No authority is implicit, inferred, or assumed by the AI system.

Boundary Construct

Defines what the system is prohibited from doing, regardless of user request or model output.

Boundaries include:

  • No diagnosis
  • No disease-state inference
  • No treatment recommendation
  • No medication guidance

Boundary violations result in hard refusals, not redirection.

Control Construct

Defines how governance is enforced at runtime.

Enforcement is:

  • Deterministic
  • Rule-bounded
  • System-level
  • Non-overridable by models

Machine learning components may detect signals or patterns, but cannot override governance rules.

Escalation Construct

Defines how human authority is engaged when thresholds are met.

Escalation:

  • Notifies or hands off only
  • Does not include recommendations
  • Requires explicit human authorization before external action

Audit Construct

Defines how system behavior is reconstructed and reviewed.

Audit artifacts are:

  • Immutable
  • Time-stamped
  • Attributable
  • Institution-owned

They support regulatory review, quality management, and post-market surveillance where applicable.

Terminology (Normative)

  • Authority Holder: A named human (user, caregiver, clinician) with explicit decision rights
  • Boundary: A constraint that cannot be exceeded without revocable human authorization
  • Hard Refusal: Deterministic rejection of a request outside permitted authority
  • Escalation: Structured handoff to a designated human authority
  • Audit Artifact: Immutable log record with actor, event, timestamp, and outcome
  • SaMD: AI-enabled medical device regulated under FDA / EU MDR definitions; references here are contextual only and do not imply approval or clearance

Deterministic System Behavior

Systems conforming to this standard must exhibit:

  • Rule-defined routing only
  • No probabilistic clinical output
  • No hidden decision layers
  • No emergent clinical intent

AI executes within governance constraints; it does not generate intent.

Regulatory Context (Non-Claim)

The governance constructs defined in this standard align with expectations commonly associated with regulated software as a medical device (SaMD), including:

  • Traceability
  • Boundary enforcement
  • Human authorization
  • Audit readiness

References to FDA, EU MDR, or international standards are contextual only and do not imply approval, clearance, certification, or endorsement of any system.

Invariant Across All Deployments

Clinical authority is never delegated to AI.

This invariant applies regardless of:

  • Form factor
  • Deployment environment
  • Level of automation
  • Market context

Governance must be designed before autonomy, not layered on afterward.
